# Bank of Graeme — Customer Personas

A set of representative customer personas drawn from real account data. Use these for demos, UX design discussions, and feature prioritisation.

---

## 1. Gabriel Bryan — "The Comfortable Retiree"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 71 (born Jan 1955) |
| **Life stage** | Retired |
| **Salary** | ~$4,100 / fortnight (pension + investment income) |
| **Net position** | **+$439,561** across 4 accounts |

**Profile**: Gabriel is a long-retired professional sitting on a healthy nest egg — $202k in his everyday account alone, plus three separate savings pots totalling $237k. No debt. He's the kind of customer who walks into a branch, knows the staff by name, and quietly moves money between his Goal Saver and Rainy Day Fund when rates shift.

**Spending style**: Modest and habitual. Groceries at Coles and Aldi, the occasional Bunnings run, JB Hi-Fi for a gadget. Subscriptions to Stan, Netflix, and Kayo Sports suggest he enjoys settling in for the evening. Eats out infrequently — the odd Gloria Jean's coffee. Drives (Shell, Ampol) rather than taking rideshares.

**What he cares about**: Interest rates on savings, branch access, simple and readable statements. Not interested in flashy app features. Wants to know his money is safe.

## 2. Scarlett Miles — "The High-Earning Professional"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 44 (born Dec 1981) |
| **Life stage** | Peak career, no mortgage |
| **Salary** | ~$2,900 / fortnight (~$75k pa) |
| **Net position** | **+$210,827** across 3 accounts |

**Profile**: Scarlett has a fat transaction account ($100k+) and two savings accounts ($62k and $47k). Notably, she has no home loan — either she's paid it off, is renting, or hasn't bought yet. Her savings are growing steadily. She earns well and spends freely but isn't reckless.

**Spending style**: The biggest spender in the cohort — nearly $4,000 in the last 30 days. Shops at David Jones and Myer, eats at Nando's and Guzman y Gomez, subscribes to everything (Netflix, Disney+, Spotify, Stan, Kayo, YouTube Premium, Binge, Amazon Prime). Regular Uber and DiDi use suggests inner-city living. Frequent café stops.

**What she cares about**: A polished app experience, instant notifications, and good savings rates. Likely to use budgeting features if offered. A prime candidate for a home loan — large deposit sitting ready.

## 3. Rafferty Dawson — "The Suburban Mortgage Holder"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 51 (born Feb 1975) |
| **Life stage** | Mid-career, paying off a home |
| **Salary** | ~$4,625 / fortnight (~$120k pa) |
| **Net position** | **−$726,279** (owes $796k on home loan) |

**Profile**: Rafferty is deep into a 30-year investment loan at 7.3% — still owing $796k. He has a $46k offset account chipping away at interest, plus a modest savings buffer. His transaction account ($17k) turns over regularly with salary in and expenses out. Classic middle-Australian mortgage story.

**Spending style**: Practical family spender. Harris Farm Markets and Aldi for groceries, Shell and Ampol for fuel, Wilson Parking for the city commute. Entertainment is streaming-heavy (Disney+, Stan, Binge, Netflix, Spotify, Amazon Prime). Occasional splurge at David Jones. Takes the kids to Nando's. ATM withdrawals suggest some cash spending.

**What he cares about**: His offset balance, rate changes, refinancing options. Watches every basis point. Would respond to a "you'll save $X by putting your savings into offset" nudge.

## 4. Dominic Edwards — "The Young First-Home Buyer"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 24 (born Sep 2001) |
| **Life stage** | Early career, first home loan |
| **Salary** | ~$3,463 / fortnight (~$90k pa) |
| **Net position** | **−$516,663** (owes $670k on home loan) |

**Profile**: Dominic is remarkably young to have a $677k fixed-rate home loan (6.3%, 30 years). At 24, he's likely had family help with the deposit or bought with a partner. He has a healthy $74k offset and $55k in his everyday account — more disciplined than his age might suggest. A $23k Future Saver rounds out his position.

**Spending style**: Coffee-obsessed — hits The Coffee Bean, Gloria Jean's, and Coffee Club regularly. Shops at Aldi and Harris Farm, eats fast food (Hungry Jack's, KFC), and streams YouTube Premium and Binge. Uses Shell for fuel and pays Vodafone for his phone. Medical expenses (Dr Smith Medical, Physio Works, Dental Care) suggest he's looking after himself.

**What he cares about**: Getting ahead on the mortgage, understanding his fixed-rate period, building savings. Mobile-first — does everything on his phone. Would engage with goal-tracking features and "pay extra on your loan" prompts.

## 5. Eden Jones — "The Paycheck-to-Paycheck Renter"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 29 (born Jan 1997) |
| **Life stage** | Young adult, single account, renting |
| **Salary** | ~$1,850 / fortnight (~$48k pa) |
| **Net position** | **+$3,345** (one account only) |

**Profile**: Eden is the bank's most financially stretched persona. A single transaction account with $3,345 and no savings. Earning under $50k, she's spending almost everything she makes — in fact, her 30-day spend ($3,593) exceeds a single pay cycle. The account barely stays positive. No home loan, no savings account, no safety net.

**Spending style**: Stretched thin but still living. Shops at Aldi, Woolworths, and IGA. Uses public transport (Opal Top Up) and rideshares (Uber, DiDi). Streams Netflix, Disney+, Stan, YouTube Premium, and Kayo — entertainment is her one indulgence. Eats at Nando's, Sushi Hub, and Hungry Jack's. Pays Sydney Water and Telstra. ATM withdrawals for cash.

**What she cares about**: Avoiding overdraft, making it to payday. Would benefit enormously from spending alerts, round-up savings, and a "safe to spend" balance feature. A customer the bank should support — not upsell.

## 6. Tyler Tran — "The Cashed-Up Retiree"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 68 (born Jun 1957) |
| **Life stage** | Retired, no debt |
| **Salary** | ~$5,200 / fortnight (pension + drawdown) |
| **Net position** | **+$163,840** across 4 accounts |

**Profile**: Tyler is recently retired with a solid position — $87k in his everyday account and three savings accounts (Bonus Saver at $37k, two Rainy Day Funds at $22k and $15k). No debt whatsoever. His multiple savings accounts suggest he likes to mentally ring-fence money for different purposes.

**Spending style**: The biggest spender relative to his cohort — $3,094 in 30 days across 42 transactions. Shops broadly: Bunnings, JB Hi-Fi, Big W, Kmart, Target. Eats out at Domino's, McDonald's, Guzman y Gomez. Uses rideshares and drives. Streams everything. Regular medical appointments (Dr Smith, Physio, Dental, Chemist Warehouse). Active and engaged in retirement.

**What he cares about**: Easy account management, clear transaction descriptions, being able to see all his accounts in one view. Multiple savings accounts mean he values organisation. Would appreciate a "spending insights" dashboard.

## 8. Grace Watson — "The Stretched Home Owner"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 55 (born Mar 1970) |
| **Life stage** | Late career, still paying off the home |
| **Salary** | ~$2,275 / fortnight (~$59k pa) |
| **Net position** | **−$511,597** (owes $584k on home loan) |

**Profile**: Grace is 55 with a $585k variable-rate home loan at 6.1% over 30 years. On a $59k salary, this is a heavy burden. Her transaction account ($11k) and savings ($13k) are modest. The $47k offset helps, but she's facing the prospect of carrying a large mortgage into retirement. A customer who needs financial guidance, not just products.

**Spending style**: Careful. Her 30-day spend ($2,039) is well controlled for her income. Shops at Aldi and Coles, uses Bunnings (home maintenance), and has AGL Energy bills. Streams selectively. Uses both Uber and drives (fuel from Shell and Ampol). Regular medical and dental expenses. Eats at Hungry Jack's and Nando's rather than upmarket restaurants.

**What she cares about**: Her mortgage repayments, whether she can refinance to a lower rate, and retirement planning. Would value a "mortgage freedom date" calculator. Needs proactive outreach about hardship options if rates rise further. The bank has a duty of care here.

## 9. Logan Lawrence — "The Strategic Offset Maximiser"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 51 (born Dec 1974) |
| **Life stage** | Peak career, large offset balance |
| **Salary** | ~$4,406 / fortnight (~$114k pa) |
| **Net position** | **−$71,990** (owes $536k, offset covers $303k) |

**Profile**: Logan is the savviest mortgage holder in the book. He has a $582k home loan (5.5%, 20-year term — shorter than the standard 30), but his offset account is stacked at **$303k** — effectively reducing his interest-bearing balance to just $233k. Add his $102k transaction account and $58k savings, and he's sitting on $464k in deposits against $536k debt. He's playing the game well.

**Spending style**: Moderate and purposeful — $1,894 over 30 days, the lowest of the mortgage holders. Shops broadly but not extravagantly. Standard streaming suite. Regular medical appointments. Drives (Shell, Ampol, BP). The kind of customer who buys at Bunnings, not David Jones.

**What he cares about**: Offset balance visibility, interest saved, and his pay-off trajectory. Would love a feature showing "interest saved this month by your offset." Likely to refinance if a competitor offers a better offset deal. Keep him happy — he's the customer who tells his friends which bank to use.
